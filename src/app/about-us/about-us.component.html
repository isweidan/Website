Guest Speaker

Rahul G. Krishnan
Bias in healthcare data

Rahul G. Krishnan is an Assistant Professor of Computer Science and Medicine (Laboratory Medicine and Pathobiology). He is a member of the Vector Institute where he holds a CIFAR AI Chair. His research has developed new algorithms for probabilistic inference, and applied machine learning to problems in healthcare such as modeling disease progression and risk stratification. He received his MS from New York University and his PhD in computer science from MIT in 2020. His research has been published in top machine learning venues such as NeurIPS, ICML, AISTATS and AAAI.

Judges

Dean Christopher Yip
https://www.engineering.utoronto.ca/about/office-of-the-dean/christopher-yip-dean/

Professor Christopher Yip began his term as Dean of the Faculty of Applied Science & Engineering on July 2, 2019, after serving two years as Associate Vice-President in the University's International Partnerships Office. Dean Yip received his B.A.Sc. in Chemical Engineering & Applied Chemistry from the University of Toronto in 1988 and his Ph.D. in Chemical Engineering from the University of Minnesota in 1996. He joined the Department of Chemical Engineering & Applied Chemistry in 1997 and was promoted to Associate Professor in 2002 and to Full Professor in 2007. Dean Yip is a leading scholar in the field of single-molecule biophysics and a faculty member with the Department of Chemical Engineering & Applied Chemistry, the Department of Biochemistry and the Institute of Biomedical Engineering. He is a Principal Investigator with the Donnelly Centre for Cellular & Biomolecular Research at the University of Toronto. As a former director of the Institute of Biomedical Engineering (BME), he provided leadership to more than 100 research engineers and scientists engaged in discovery and product development in the areas of neuroscience and sensory stimulation, biomaterials and tissue engineering, molecular systems biology and nanotechnology, as well as medical device and drug delivery system design.

Michael Guerzhoy

Michael Guerzhoy is an Assistant Professor, Teaching Stream in the Division of Engineering Science and in the Dept. of Mechanical and Industrial Engineering at the University of Toronto, and an Affiliate Scientist at the Li Ka Shing Knowledge Institute at St. Michael's Hospital in Toronto. Previously, Michael taught data science at Princeton University, worked on AI for healthcare at St. Michael's Hospital, worked on 3D pose recognition at Inria Grenoble, France, and worked on computer vision algorithms for printers and scanners at Seiko Epson Corporation. Michael co-designed and taught the first iteration of several courses: SML310 Research Projects in Data Science and SML480 Pedagogy of Data Science at Princeton University; C4M Computing for Medicine at the University of Toronto; and CSC180 Introduction to Computer Programming in Python at the University of Toronto. Michael is serving as the co-chair of the Symposium on Educational Advances in Artificial Intelligence in 2021-2023. Michael's research interests are in machine learning, computer vision, the applications of data science in healthcare, applied statistics, and the pedagogy of computer science, data science, and artificial intelligence. Michael won the best paper award at the Canadian Conference on Artificial Intelligence in 2014, and won the University of Toronto Computer Science Student Union Teaching Award in 2016.

Vikash Ruhil

Mr. Vikash Ruhil earned a Bachelor of Technology in Computer Science from MDU university and M.S. in Data Mining and Predictive Analytics from St. John’s University. Mr. Ruhil specialized in data science, econometrics, credit risk, and creditworthiness using AI models, building complex statistical and mathematical models in pricing, residual value analysis, and predictive analytics models on finance and healthcare during his graduate studies. He is a senior data scientist with 10 years of experience in the technology industry and major fields in financial technology, pricing, product marketing finance and credit risk domain. He worked as Head of Data Science for Deserve. He built data science and machine learning algorithms to measure creditworthiness using alternative data. He built a credit underwriting engine and domino credit scoring system which uses various attributes such as credit files, credit scores, income and cash flow underwriting. At VMware, He worked as a senior data scientist developing data science and machine learning models for cloud management products.

Malcolm Thorburn

I am Professor of Law; I hold the Chair in the Legal, Ethical and Cultural Implications of Technological Innovation; and I am the Associate Dean, Graduate Programs at the Faculty of Law, University of Toronto. I studied philosophy at U of T (BAH) and at the University of Pennsylvania in Philadelphia (MA), and I studied law at U of T (JD) and at Columbia Law School in New York (LLM, SJD). I have worked at universities around the world, including Queen’s University, Kingston, Canada; Sciences Po, Paris; the Australian National University; Ludwig Maximilians University in Munich, Germany; and the University of Oxford, UK. I write about criminal law and policing from the perspective of constitutional law and political theory. I have published articles in journals around the world and I have edited two books: The Philosophical Foundations of Constitutional Law (with David Dyzenhaus) and The Dignity of Law (with Dwight Newman). I have never judged a hackathon before, but I am looking forward to it!
